The first full moon of 2026 rises on January 3rd. Known as the Wolf Moon in many northern cultures, this bright lunar phase will dominate the night sky. While the full moon washes out fainter objects, it's a beautiful sight and perfect for lunar observation with binoculars or telescopes.

Under ideal conditions, observers can see up to 40 meteors per hour. However, this year the full Wolf Moon will significantly reduce visibility of fainter meteors. üî≠ Monthly Astronomy Talk: The (Life and) Death of Stars

Date: Friday, January 3, 2026

Time: 7:30 PM - 9:30 PM (Social time at 7:30 PM, talk begins at 8:00 PM)

Location: Houge Park, Twilight Drive & White Oaks Avenue, San Jose

Talk: The (Life and) Death of Stars: Where the Elements Around Us Form

From the oxygen we breathe to the gold in our jewelry and the calcium in our bones, the majority of periodic table elements that make up our world are forged by stars. This talk explores how the universe transforms simple hydrogen into the rich diversity of atoms that build planets, life, and everything we see around us.

Beginning their lives in vast clouds of gas and ending in massive explosions, we will discover how each star in the night sky creates and scatters ingredients for new worlds throughout their evolution. By tracing this stellar alchemy, we uncover how true it is to say that we are all just stardust.

Speaker: Natalie LeBaron is a 4th-year PhD student in the Astronomy Department at UC Berkeley. She studies the visible and near infrared light from supernova explosions of massive stars to learn about the elements and molecules they synthesize and interact with. Natalie is passionate about science outreach, mentorship, and removing barriers within STEM education.

Date: Saturday, January 4, 2026

Time: 1:30 PM - 3:30 PM

Location: Houge Park, 3972 Twilight Dr, San Jose

It's there for us year round, lighting our days and providing energy for our lives, so maybe it's time to give it a closer look. Join SJAA for amazing and detailed views of the Sun through special telescopes that will keep your eyeballs perfectly safe!

What You'll See:

  • White-light telescopes with solar filters revealing sunspots
  • Hydrogen-alpha telescopes showing prominences (solar flares) and intricate texture within the Sun's chromosphere
  • Learn about how the Sun works and how magnetic fields drive solar activity

A few minutes after 1:30 PM, we'll have a short, informal introductory talk. At other times, you can enjoy the views and ask questions about the Sun, telescopes, or astronomy in general. We often have a station where you can get a better feel for the huge scale of our solar system!

Bring Your Own Telescope: If you have a properly filtered white light or H-alpha telescope and want to share views with others, please arrive at 1:00 PM or earlier to set up.
